Introduction · Chapter One

Where This
Research Began

For most of my life, I knew I was Māori — but I didn't publicly claim it. I felt I wasn't the right skin colour. Couldn't speak te reo. Had been told not to trust Māori.

That silence is what colonisation does. This research started as one question: what happens when AI fills those gaps with the wrong answers?

The Gap

Only 21% of Māori report conversational te reo proficiency — yet generative AI is already embedded in education, health, and government.

The Scale Problem

For widely spoken languages, AI brings access. For te reo Māori, it brings confident-sounding errors at scale.

Whakamā

The shame of reconnecting — created by colonisation — is real. AI that gets te reo wrong reinforces the silence that was already there.

The Goal

One million te reo Māori speakers by 2040. AI has arrived before the governance needed to make it safe has been built.

Literature Review · Chapter Two

The History We
Cannot Ignore

Te reo Māori did not arrive at this moment of digital vulnerability by accident.

1867
Native Schools Act

Te reo banned from formal education. Children punished for speaking their language.

1900–60
Fluency Collapses

~95% fluency to 25% within two generations.

1982
Kōhanga Reo

Community-led immersion — Māori fighting back.

1987
Māori Language Act

Te reo officially recognised. Language Commission established.

Today
Fragile Recovery

21% conversational. 1M speaker goal by 2040. AI has arrived before governance has caught up.

AI Misrepresents Te Reo

Gender-neutral 'ia' given colonial gender roles. Whakapapa stripped from terms that carry it. Errors normalised at scale.

A Global Indigenous Parallel

Canada, Australia, Brazil, and Pacific communities face the same fight. The UN Declaration states data must be community-controlled.

Digital Colonialism

AI can replicate extractive patterns of colonisation at unprecedented speed — without anyone intending harm.

Findings · Chapter Four

Five
Interconnected
Themes

These themes emerged from ten interviews and twenty survey responses — deeply interconnected, forming one coherent story.

01

AI as a Crossroads of Risk and Opportunity

AI is a critical decision point — choices made now will shape future outcomes. Disengagement is not safety; it shifts control to others. A double digital divide emerged: not just lack of access, but lack of AI literacy.

"We're at a crossroads. We can either say AI is bad, let's ignore it, and then we will be recolonised — but in a way that no one can comprehend right now."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025
02

Data Sovereignty as a Non-Negotiable Foundation

Every participant returned to data sovereignty as the starting point. Before AI can support Māori language or culture, the question of who owns and controls the data must be resolved first.

"We get brought in at the eighth hour — everything's already happened. The opportunity to influence and co-design has been and gone."

— Research participant, interview 2025
03

Governance Must Be Tikanga-Based, Not Tokenistic

Having Māori present is not the same as having Māori governance. Tokenistic inclusion can actively cause harm — it creates the appearance of cultural safety without any real shift in decision-making power.

"It needs someone with the technology, the te reo Māori knowledge, and the knowledge of Te Tiriti. And it's probably not always just one person."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025
04

Community Authority and Co-Design as the Only Viable Path

Community is not a stakeholder — it is the source of authority. Technology can be a pathway into deeper human connection, but it cannot substitute for it. The goal is always to bring people to the marae, to the kaumātua, to the living language.

"The technology can help, but it will never replace that feeling of connection."

— Smith, interview 2025
05

The Persistent Gap Between Principle and Practice

The principles for ethical AI already exist. The challenge is applying them. This is not a gap of intent — it is a gap of structure, resourcing, and practical guidance.

"A lot of people talked about wanting to implement Māori data sovereignty — but they didn't know how to operationalise it."

— Research participant, interview 2025
"We are a bicultural country… if we did a little bit more work about what that means, we could actually move faster."— Young, C., interview 2025
Deep Dive · Findings

Data Sovereignty in Practice

Classifying Māori data before any AI system is built or procured.

Noa Open / Unrestricted

Publicly available data. Can be digitised and used within AI systems with appropriate care.

Noa
Tapu Controlled / Restricted

Sensitive cultural data to be kept within Aotearoa. Requires clear authority over who may access it and how.

Tapu
Rāhui Sacred / Must Not Be Digitised

Whakapapa, sacred knowledge, medicines, and family-held knowledge. Never digitised or fed into AI systems.

Rāhui

"We can't rely on other people's principles. We need to create our own, and we need tino rangatiratanga, mana motuhake to do that."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025

Interview Findings · Deep Dive

AI & Indigenous
Storytelling

Two questions drew the most honest and complex responses across all interviews.

Question 1

What does success look like in 10 years?

Participants envisioned communities owning their own AI systems with data on community-held infrastructure — modelled on Te Hiku Media. Legislative reform and Indigenous-led AI careers featured in almost every response.

Sovereignty & Ownership

Each major iwi with their own AI capability — built by and for their community, with full kaitiakitanga.

Legislative Reform

Copyright Act, Privacy Act, and digital harm laws described as decades out of date. Success means those gaps are closed.

"Not one indigenous sovereign AI — we will have multiple, owned 100% by indigenous communities, not organisations."

— Lee Timutimu, interview 2025
Question 2

"How would you feel if AI told your story to future generations?"

Strong Desire for Control

Stories are owned at the whānau level — control stays with family, not a machine or organisation.

Openness — With Conditions

Some were comfortable in principle if they had contributed directly to what was captured.

Fear of Distortion

Different whānau hold different versions of the same person. Present multiple perspectives rather than flattening them.

Scepticism About Authenticity

AI can archive stories — but cannot replace a person telling their story at a tangi, in the moment.

Multiple Truths as Valid

"That's quite a Western construct — we always talk about there being multiple truths." AI's drive toward singular outputs conflicts with this worldview.

Generational Perspective

What feels uncomfortable now, future generations may consider completely normal. The real question is what our mokopuna will think is ordinary.

"The stories are still there, still correct and still precious. I'm not feared of AI anymore."

— Francie Diver, interview 2025

From the Research · Community Voice

Community Conditions
for Trust

Six minimum requirements before communities can genuinely trust AI with their language and stories.

Accuracy Must Be Near-Perfect

Cultural and linguistic errors carry real harm — misrepresenting whakapapa or tikanga in ways that may not be immediately visible.

Māori Control

Genuine decision-making authority — not advisory roles. Control over data, access, and the ability to shut the system down.

Transparency

Communities need to understand how AI works with their data — in plain language. Opacity is incompatible with trust.

Safety from Harm

Active prevention of misuse — deepfakes, fake Māori identities, and the weaponisation of cultural signals.

Intergenerational Protection

Whatever is built must consider what future generations will inherit — not just present use.

Consent & Regular Review

Ongoing, contextual consent — not a one-time sign-off. Communities retain the right to withdraw at any point.

Practical Guidance · From the Research

Before You Use AI

For anyone using AI to engage with te reo Māori or Māori cultural knowledge — whether reconnecting with language, working in an organisation, or getting a translation.

Question 1

Do you know what the tool is doing with your input?

Your input may be stored and used to improve the model. Many AI tools were trained on Māori language content collected without community knowledge or consent.

Ask: Does this tool have a clear data policy? Was it trained with Māori community consent?

Question 2

Is the output correct — or just confident?

AI sounds certain even when it's wrong. For those reconnecting with their language, a mistranslation can fill gaps left by colonisation with something that feels right but isn't real.

Ask: Have I validated this with a fluent speaker? Am I treating AI as a starting point or a final answer?

Question 3

Is what you're sharing noa, tapu, or rāhui?

Not all Māori data is the same. Noa — publicly available. Tapu — sensitive, requires authority. Rāhui — sacred, must never be digitised or shared with AI systems.

If you're unsure which category something falls into, that uncertainty is a signal to pause.

Question 4

Are you using AI as a bridge — or letting it become the destination?

AI can make te reo more accessible. But it cannot replace the warmth of a person, a marae, or a kaumātua. If it's helping you get there, it's working. If it's replacing those things, that's worth questioning.

"The technology can help, but it will never replace that feeling of connection."

— Smith, interview 2025
Question 5

Are you informed enough to push back if something feels wrong?

You don't need to be a technologist — but you need enough understanding to notice when something doesn't feel right, and to make decisions about what you share.

"We're at a crossroads. If we don't grasp it now, we will be recolonised — but in a way that no one can comprehend right now."

— Karaitiana Taiuru, interview 2025
